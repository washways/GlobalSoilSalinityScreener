What is this tool?

A satellite-powered screening layer that identifies where environmental conditions favour salinity accumulation.

This is not a direct salinity measurement map. Instead, it's a susceptibility surface that highlights areas where soil texture, drainage conditions, terrain, climate, and hydrological setting combine to make salt accumulation or persistence more likely.

The design is grounded in peer-reviewed salinity and hydropedology literature. In data-scarce regions, where field surveys are expensive and time-consuming, this tool provides a first-pass screening to identify zones of concern, focus limited resources, or inform policy decisions about water quality monitoring.

💡 Key insight: Salinity patterns are driven by multiple factors working together—no single variable tells the full story. That's why the model combines five complementary indicators of environmental setting.

How It Works

Five environmental factors, each normalized to 0–1 and weighted by scientific evidence.

The model combines five independently measured environmental factors. Each represents a different aspect of the physical conditions that affect salt storage and movement: 

Fine-textured subsoil (32%)  ────┐
Weak drainage (23%)  ────────────┤   Weighted   Terrain  
Flatness (20%)  ────────────────┤→  Sum  →  Suppress → Score
Climatic dryness (17%)  ────────┤   (0–1)     (0–1)
Near persistent water (8%)  ────┘

Default weights sum to 100 and are intentionally interpretable rather than data-driven. In a future version, the strongest upgrade would be to calibrate these weights against field electrical conductivity (EC) measurements.

The Five Components

What each factor measures and why it matters.

32%

Fine-textured Subsoil

Question: Does the subsoil contain fine materials?

Fine soils (clay, silt) slow water percolation and increase capillary rise—conditions where salts can accumulate or move upward. Measured at 100–200 cm depth from SoilGrids.

23%

Weak Drainage

Question: Is the soil poorly drained?

Poor drainage reduces flushing of accumulated salts. This is derived from the inverse of a texture-drainage proxy—coarser soils drain freely, finer soils drain poorly.

20%

Flatness

Question: Is the terrain flat?

Flat, low-relief terrain tends to be depositional and poorly drained. Steep terrain sheds water as runoff. Derived from SRTM slope by inverting the signal.

17%

Climatic Dryness

Question: Is the climate dry?

Salts concentrate where evaporation exceeds effective leaching. Measured via TerraClimate's long-term precipitation-to-evapotranspiration ratio.

8%

Near Persistent Water

Question: Is the area near surface water?

Proximity to persistent water can indicate lowlands, floodplains, or hydrologically connected zones where salinity processes occur. Can generate false positives if over-emphasized.

Component Visibility in the App

Each component is available as a toggleable layer in the map's Layers panel (top right). This allows you to:

  • Understand what drives the final susceptibility score at any location
  • Verify that the model is making sense locally
  • Cross-reference components with field knowledge
  • Explore component behavior across your region

Score Interpretation

What different score ranges mean.

The final output is normalized to 0–1. On the map, red indicates highest susceptibility, and blue indicates lowest.

Score Range Interpretation What to Do
0.0–0.2 Very Low Low combined evidence. De-prioritise unless strong local knowledge suggests otherwise.
0.2–0.4 Low Some signal, but weak. Cross-reference with field EC or borehole data.
0.4–0.6 Medium Promising. Identify which component(s) drive the score and verify locally.
0.6–0.8 High Strong screening signal. Good candidate for targeted survey or testing.
0.8–1.0 Exceptional Top-tier signal. Merge with field observations for validation.
⚠️ Important: A high susceptibility score is a hypothesis, not a guarantee. Always verify results with field salinity measurements (EC, SAR, TDS) before making decisions about water management, drilling, or irrigation.

How to Use the App

Step-by-step guide to get the most from the tool.

  1. Zoom in to your area of interest at zoom level ≥ 9 (district or catchment scale recommended)
  2. Select a country using the dropdown on the left, or click on the map
  3. (Optional) Adjust weights using the sliders if you have local knowledge about which factors matter most. Weights are bounded and rebalanced automatically to sum to 100.
  4. Click "Reload map" to apply your changes and recompute the susceptibility layer
  5. Toggle component layers using the Layers panel (top right) to understand what drives the final score
  6. Click on any location to see a detailed diagnostic popup showing the score and component breakdown
  7. (Optional) Enable Auto-stretch for better colour contrast when zoomed in to local scale
💡 Tip: Large countries may take 30–60 seconds to reload. This is normal—the app is computing thousands of pixels country-wide on Google's servers. Use the Layers panel to explore components without recomputing.

Limitations & Caveats

What this tool is not, and what it cannot do.

Limitation Implication
Screening tool only Not a substitute for field EC survey, hydrogeological investigation, geophysical survey, or formal regulatory compliance mapping
No field calibration Weights are literature-informed but not fitted to measured groundwater salinity, electrical conductivity, or sodium adsorption ratio. Future versions should include field EC calibration.
Resolution ceiling Limited by coarsest inputs (SoilGrids at 250 m). Cannot resolve meter-scale soil variation or local hydrogeology.
Static climate proxy TerraClimate moisture ratio represents a 60+ year average, not year-specific recharge or current drought conditions.
No water quality component High susceptibility ≠ hazardous salinity. This tool does not assess sodium adsorption ratio (SAR), fluoride, arsenic, or other trace contaminants.
Context-dependence Relationships between predictors and observed salinity vary by aquifer type, irrigation intensity, climate regime, and geology.
Missing inputs Does not include groundwater depth, aquifer lithology, irrigation extent, valley-bottom metrics (HAND), or borehole productivity data.
⚠️ Bottom line: This is a hypothesis-generating tool for first-pass screening in data-scarce regions. Use it to identify zones worthy of further investigation, but always ground decisions in field data and local expertise.

Recommended Next Steps

After using the screening layer.

  • Collect field EC data at high- and low-susceptibility locations to validate the model
  • Interview local water users about known salinity zones, borehole failures, or agricultural salinity impacts
  • Cross-reference with borehole logs to check depth-to-water, aquifer lithology, and historical water quality
  • Integrate with hydrogeological maps (aquifer type, fault lines, recharge zones) if available
  • Share your ground-truth observations with the WASHways team to help calibrate future versions. See CONTRIBUTING.md.
